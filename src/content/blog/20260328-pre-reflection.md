## An Unexpected Start

At first, it seemed almost impossible for me to complete this 15-minute presentation with my partner, Zhu Zexin.

Looking back on the first class, I can still remember the moment when I randomly—but unfortunately—picked the slip with a big, lonely “1” on it. We were the first group, and that immediately made me nervous. Just like last term’s oral test, being first meant that we would have very limited time to prepare. Unlike the other groups, we would not even have the chance to learn from our classmates’ performances beforehand.

The first challenge was choosing a topic. I brainstormed on my own and even sought some help from a large language model. We came up with many possible ideas, such as Shakespeare, Harry Potter, British humour, and the weather in Great Britain. However, finding a topic that both of us were familiar with turned out to be much harder than expected. After a great deal of discussion, we finally found a balance and decided to take English football as our entry point, hoping to explore British culture through a subject that was both distinctive and engaging.

## Preparation, Cooperation, and the Presentation

Since I did not know much about English football, Zhu took on a large share of the work. He searched for video clips, designed the slides, and wrote the first drafts of the script. He also left the interaction parts to me, which I truly appreciated.

For my part, I focused on improving the script. I read it carefully, compared it with the slides, thought about how to attract the audience’s attention, and rewrote many parts in my own words so that I could present them more naturally. It was a long and demanding process, but I can honestly say that I tried my best and brought out the best in myself.

Then came the day of the presentation. To be honest, I was still not fully confident, and I made some oral mistakes from time to time. As I stepped onto the stage, I felt nervous. Yet when I saw the eyes of our teacher and classmates fixed on us, waiting for our performance, that nervousness gradually turned into courage.

When it was my turn to speak, I walked to the front and began my part. Our topic was somewhat professional, so I tried to make it livelier by adding guessing games and a cultural quiz. However, the audience did not seem truly involved, and few people were willing to answer. At that moment, I felt a little disappointed. Later, I realized that this was not the audience’s fault. The real problem was that we had not designed those interactive parts well enough. They were not clear, attractive, or easy enough to join in. This experience taught me that interaction in a presentation is not simply about asking questions; it is about designing questions that people genuinely want to answer.

## Reflection and Growth

After the final words faded, I bowed and stood before our teacher, waiting for comments and suggestions. Although I remained silent on the outside, I was actually eager to hear her feedback.

Our teacher spoke gently and patiently, pointing out both our strengths and our weaknesses. More importantly, she gave us practical suggestions for improvement. From ways of engaging the audience more effectively to advice on how to design comprehension questions based on video clips, I learned many useful techniques for giving a better presentation.

So what did this experience mean to me? In my summary for last term’s English course, I wrote that oral speaking and lecturing were still among my weaknesses. This time, I tried to face that weakness directly. Pushing myself forward was not easy, but the process itself was meaningful and rewarding. For the first time, I gained a basic understanding of English football. More importantly, I stepped forward, expressed myself in front of others, and showed the value of teamwork with my partner.

It was only a beginning, but it was a good one. I believe I can still do more—to become more passionate, more energetic, more open, and, above all, more aware of my own potential.
